How to Calculate Discount: Sale Price Formulas Made Easy

Shopping sales can be confusing — is 30% off a good deal? What's the actual price after a "buy one get one 50% off" promotion? This guide teaches you how to calculate any discount quickly so you can make smart purchasing decisions.

The Basic Discount Formula

Sale Price = Original Price × (1 − Discount% ÷ 100)

Or broken into two steps:

  1. Discount Amount = Original Price × (Discount% ÷ 100)
  2. Sale Price = Original Price − Discount Amount

Example

A $80 jacket is 25% off:

  • Discount: $80 × 0.25 = $20
  • Sale price: $80 − $20 = $60
  • Shortcut: $80 × 0.75 = $60

Finding the Discount Percentage

If you know the original and sale price but want to find the discount percentage:

Discount% = [(Original − Sale Price) ÷ Original] × 100

Example

A laptop was $1,200, now $900. What's the discount?

  • Savings: $1,200 − $900 = $300
  • Percentage: ($300 ÷ $1,200) × 100 = 25% off

Finding the Original Price

You see a sale price and want to know what it was before the discount:

Original Price = Sale Price ÷ (1 − Discount% ÷ 100)

Example

A shirt costs $45 after a 40% discount. What was the original price?

  • $45 ÷ (1 − 0.40) = $45 ÷ 0.60 = $75

Double (Stacked) Discounts

When two discounts apply sequentially (e.g., 20% off + extra 10% off), they do NOT simply add up to 30%:

Final Price = Original × (1 − First%) × (1 − Second%)

Example

$100 item with 20% off, then an extra 10% off:

  • After first discount: $100 × 0.80 = $80
  • After second discount: $80 × 0.90 = $72
  • Total effective discount: 28% (not 30%!)

The combined discount formula: 1 − (1 − 0.20)(1 − 0.10) = 1 − 0.72 = 28%

Discount + Tax: Which Comes First?

In most places, sales tax is calculated on the discounted price (after the discount is applied):

  1. Original price: $100
  2. After 30% discount: $100 × 0.70 = $70
  3. Add 8% tax: $70 × 1.08 = $75.60

This is better for you than if tax were applied first!

Is It Really a Good Deal?

Tips to evaluate whether a discount is worth it:

  • Calculate cost per use — A $200 coat at 50% off ($100) worn 100 times = $1/wear
  • Compare unit prices — "Buy 3 for $10" vs "$4 each" — the bulk deal is only better if you need all 3
  • Check the original price — Some retailers inflate the "original" price before sales
  • Consider opportunity cost — Money spent on a deal is still money spent
  • Set a price threshold — Decide the maximum you'd pay before seeing the discount

Quick Mental Math for Discounts

  • 10% off — Move decimal left: $85 → $8.50 off → $76.50
  • 20% off — Double the 10%: $85 → $17 off → $68
  • 25% off — Divide by 4: $85 → ~$21 off → $64
  • 33% off — Divide by 3: $85 → ~$28 off → $57
  • 50% off — Halve it: $85 → $42.50

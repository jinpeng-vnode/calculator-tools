How to Calculate Loan Payment: Monthly Payment Formula Explained

Understanding how to calculate your monthly loan payment helps you budget effectively and compare loan offers. Whether you're taking out a mortgage, car loan, or personal loan, the same fundamental formula applies.

The Loan Payment Formula

The standard formula for calculating a fixed monthly loan payment is based on the amortization principle:

M = P × [r(1+r)ⁿ] ÷ [(1+r)ⁿ - 1]

Where:

  • M = Monthly payment
  • P = Principal (loan amount)
  • r = Monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12)
  • n = Total number of payments (loan term in months)

Step-by-Step Calculation Example

Let's calculate the monthly payment for a $200,000 mortgage at 6.5% annual interest over 30 years:

  1. Identify the principal (P) — $200,000
  2. Calculate monthly interest rate (r) — 6.5% ÷ 12 = 0.5417% = 0.005417
  3. Determine total payments (n) — 30 years × 12 months = 360 payments
  4. Calculate (1+r)ⁿ — (1.005417)³⁶⁰ = 6.9918
  5. Apply the formula — $200,000 × [0.005417 × 6.9918] ÷ [6.9918 - 1]
  6. Result — $200,000 × 0.03787 ÷ 5.9918 = $1,264.14/month

Understanding Total Loan Cost

Your monthly payment is just part of the picture. To understand the true cost of a loan:

  • Total amount paid = Monthly payment × Number of payments = $1,264.14 × 360 = $455,090
  • Total interest paid = Total paid - Principal = $455,090 - $200,000 = $255,090

In this example, you'd pay more in interest than the original loan amount over 30 years!

Strategies to Lower Your Monthly Payment

  • Increase your down payment — A larger down payment reduces the principal, directly lowering monthly payments
  • Extend the loan term — A 30-year term has lower monthly payments than a 15-year term (but more total interest)
  • Shop for better rates — Compare offers from multiple lenders; even 0.25% matters
  • Improve your credit score — Higher scores qualify for lower interest rates
  • Consider adjustable rates — ARM loans may start lower but carry risk of rate increases

Extra Payments: Saving on Interest

Making extra payments toward principal can dramatically reduce your total interest and loan duration. For our $200,000 example at 6.5%:

  • Adding $100/month extra saves $62,000 in interest and pays off the loan 5 years early
  • Adding $200/month extra saves $101,000 in interest and pays off 9 years early
  • One extra payment per year saves $47,000 and cuts 4 years off the term

Types of Loan Payments

Fixed-Rate Loans

The formula above applies to fixed-rate loans where the interest rate stays constant throughout the term. Your monthly payment never changes, making budgeting predictable.

Interest-Only Loans

During the interest-only period, your payment is simply:

Monthly Payment = Principal × (Annual Rate ÷ 12)

For our example: $200,000 × 0.005417 = $1,083.33/month (interest only, no principal reduction).

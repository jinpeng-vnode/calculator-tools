How to Convert Temperature: Celsius, Fahrenheit & Kelvin

Temperature conversion is one of the most common calculations in daily life, science, and cooking. Whether you're reading a recipe from another country or studying thermodynamics, knowing how to convert between Celsius, Fahrenheit, and Kelvin is essential.

The Three Temperature Scales

  • Celsius (°C) — Used worldwide for everyday temperatures. Water freezes at 0°C and boils at 100°C.
  • Fahrenheit (°F) — Used primarily in the United States. Water freezes at 32°F and boils at 212°F.
  • Kelvin (K) — Used in science. Starts at absolute zero (−273.15°C). No degree symbol is used.

Conversion Formulas

Celsius to Fahrenheit

°F = (°C × 9/5) + 32

Fahrenheit to Celsius

°C = (°F − 32) × 5/9

Celsius to Kelvin

K = °C + 273.15

Kelvin to Celsius

°C = K − 273.15

Fahrenheit to Kelvin

K = (°F − 32) × 5/9 + 273.15

Step-by-Step Examples

Example 1: Convert 25°C to Fahrenheit

  1. Multiply by 9/5: 25 × 9/5 = 45
  2. Add 32: 45 + 32 = 77°F

Example 2: Convert 98.6°F to Celsius

  1. Subtract 32: 98.6 − 32 = 66.6
  2. Multiply by 5/9: 66.6 × 5/9 = 37°C

This is normal human body temperature — a useful reference point to remember!

Example 3: Convert 100°C to Kelvin

  1. Add 273.15: 100 + 273.15 = 373.15 K

Mental Math Tricks

For quick approximate conversions without a calculator:

  • °C to °F (quick): Double the Celsius value and add 30. Example: 20°C → 40 + 30 = 70°F (actual: 68°F)
  • °F to °C (quick): Subtract 30, then divide by 2. Example: 80°F → 50 ÷ 2 = 25°C (actual: 26.7°C)
  • Remember −40: This is where Celsius and Fahrenheit are equal (−40°C = −40°F)

Common Use Cases

  • Cooking — Converting oven temperatures between American and European recipes (350°F = 177°C)
  • Travel — Understanding weather forecasts in different countries
  • Science — Lab work typically uses Celsius or Kelvin
  • Medicine — Body temperature is reported in °F in the US and °C elsewhere
  • HVAC — Thermostat settings when moving between countries

Why Do Different Scales Exist?

Daniel Fahrenheit created his scale in 1724, using brine (salt water) as 0° and human body temperature as 96°. Anders Celsius proposed his scale in 1742, based on water's freezing and boiling points. Lord Kelvin introduced the absolute scale in 1848 for scientific use, starting at the theoretical lowest possible temperature.

Today, most of the world uses Celsius for everyday purposes, while Kelvin is the SI unit for scientific measurements.

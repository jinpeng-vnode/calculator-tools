How to Calculate BMI: A Complete Step-by-Step Guide

Body Mass Index (BMI) is one of the most widely used screening tools to assess whether a person has a healthy body weight relative to their height. Whether you're tracking your fitness progress or simply curious about your health status, understanding how to calculate BMI is essential.

What Is BMI?

BMI stands for Body Mass Index. It's a numerical value derived from your weight and height that provides a quick estimate of body fat. Developed by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet in the 1830s, BMI remains a standard tool used by healthcare professionals worldwide.

While BMI doesn't directly measure body fat percentage, it serves as a reliable indicator for most people to determine if they fall within a healthy weight range.

The BMI Formula

There are two versions of the BMI formula depending on whether you use metric or imperial units:

Metric Formula

BMI = Weight (kg) ÷ Height² (m²)

Imperial Formula

BMI = [Weight (lbs) ÷ Height² (in²)] × 703

Step-by-Step BMI Calculation

Let's walk through a practical example using the metric formula:

  1. Measure your weight — For example, 70 kg
  2. Measure your height in meters — For example, 1.75 m
  3. Square your height — 1.75 × 1.75 = 3.0625
  4. Divide weight by height squared — 70 ÷ 3.0625 = 22.86

In this example, the BMI is 22.86, which falls in the normal weight category.

Imperial Example

For someone weighing 154 lbs and standing 5'9" (69 inches):

  1. Square the height: 69 × 69 = 4,761
  2. Divide weight by height squared: 154 ÷ 4,761 = 0.03234
  3. Multiply by 703: 0.03234 × 703 = 22.73

BMI Categories

The World Health Organization (WHO) defines the following BMI categories for adults:

BMI RangeCategory
Below 18.5Underweight
18.5 – 24.9Normal weight
25.0 – 29.9Overweight
30.0 – 34.9Obesity Class I
35.0 – 39.9Obesity Class II
40.0 and aboveObesity Class III

Limitations of BMI

While BMI is useful as a quick screening tool, it has several limitations:

  • Muscle mass — Athletes may have a high BMI due to muscle, not fat
  • Age and gender — BMI doesn't account for differences in body composition between men and women or age-related changes
  • Body fat distribution — BMI doesn't indicate where fat is stored (visceral vs. subcutaneous)
  • Ethnicity — Health risks at the same BMI can vary across ethnic groups

For a more complete health assessment, consider combining BMI with waist circumference measurements and consulting a healthcare professional.

Tips for a Healthy BMI

  • Maintain a balanced diet rich in fruits, vegetables, lean proteins, and whole grains
  • Engage in at least 150 minutes of moderate aerobic activity per week
  • Monitor your weight regularly but don't obsess over daily fluctuations
  • Focus on overall health markers, not just the number on the scale
